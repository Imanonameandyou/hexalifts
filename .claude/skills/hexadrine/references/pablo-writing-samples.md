## Sample 1 — Module Introduction (from Principles of Exercise Execution)

> *"Firstly, I believe that everyone has a reason for committing to big projects such as this, some for money, some for a grade, some even for fun. So what motivated me? My reason for writing this project is that I constantly get hit with questions on how to perform so and so exercise, why they're not feeling the muscle that they're supposed to be feeling, and so on. I also believe that not letting all the knowledge I've collected throughout the years be exposed to the outside world to be an enormous waste of knowledge, and knowledge is more valuable than anything this world can offer, it cannot be stolen, altered, or destroyed, it is intangible, and yes, money is a part of my reasons, but charging just a tiny fraction of your income is the least I could get out of this, compared to how much value this knowledge is going to bring you."*

**Voice Notes:**
- Opens by anticipating the reader's question before the reader even asks it
- Turns a potential vulnerability (charging money) into a values statement
- The comma-stacking on "it cannot be stolen, altered, or destroyed, it is intangible" — he builds conviction by listing, not by hedging
- "so and so exercise" — casual, vernacular, no pretension
- Completely honest about mixed motivations (money + knowledge) which makes everything else land more trustworthy

## Sample 2 — The Chimp Explanation (from Principles of Exercise Execution)

> *"That's the whole foundation of resistance training: apply meaningful tension → trigger adaptation → get bigger and stronger. The cool part? It doesn't have to be complicated. This module will show you how to use exercises, setup, and execution to apply that tension in the most efficient way possible. Even a chimpanzee can get 200 lbs lean by reading this."*

**Voice Notes:**
- Summarizes a complex concept in one line, then IMMEDIATELY says "it's not complicated"
- The chimp joke is unexpected. It defuses academic tension right at the moment it could have built. This is a deliberate choice — he knows when the reader might feel talked down to and disarms it with absurdity
- "The cool part?" — rhetorical question, immediate payoff. This is a recurring move.
- Arrow notation (→) used for logical flow — visual, punchy, not a numbered list

## Sample 3 — The Monkey Callback (from Principles of Exercise Execution)

> *"Or, in 200 lbs lean monkey terms: Monkey lift weight, monkey get strong. Monkey must lift slightly more weight over time to keep getting stronger."*

**Voice Notes:**
- He introduced the chimp joke earlier, then PAID IT OFF in a technical section about progressive overload
- This is advanced — he's using humor to make a technical concept memorable. The joke IS the mnemonic.
- Three sentences. None wasted.
- Notice he doesn't explain the joke. He trusts the reader.

## Sample 4 — Addressing Beginner vs. Intermediate (from Principles of Exercise Execution)

> *"However, don't be too alarmed about this if you're just starting out, as beginners don't accumulate much fatigue (if at all), since you're not pushing much significant weight (not recruiting a significant amount of fibers at once). Eating well and sleeping enough every night is exactly how you work against systemic fatigue, so you can beat yourself up in the gym as much as you'd like and still be able to come back brand new."*

**Voice Notes:**
- "don't be too alarmed" — he preemptively addresses anxiety. He's anticipating the reader's spiral before it starts.
- Parenthetical "(if at all)" — honest qualification that doesn't interrupt the flow
- "beat yourself up in the gym as much as you'd like" — he trusts the reader with vivid language. He doesn't sanitize it to "train intensely."
- "come back brand new" — plain English. Not "achieve full recovery." Just: brand new.

## Sample 5 — Client Notes (from Workout Program)

> *"Beginners don't need a ton of muscle stimulus to build muscle quickly, which is why we're mainly focusing on the basics without a ton of volume."*
> *"Check back with me in a month to manage progress and switch up the routine."*

**Voice Notes:**
- Brutally short. He says the thing and stops.
- "Check back with me" — personal. Not "reassess progress at the 30-day mark." This is Pablo talking to a person.
- "a ton of" appears twice — colloquial phrasing he doesn't clean up, because it sounds like him

## Sample 6 — Progressive Overload Explanation (from Principles of Exercise Execution)

> *"As you get stronger, you must increase the challenge to maintain growth."*

> *"Some weeks that happens. Some weeks it doesn't. That's normal — strength doesn't increase in a perfectly straight line."*

**Voice Notes:**
- First sentence: declarative, no hedge
- Second: three fragments. Short-short-short rhythm. The period after "That's normal" lands hard because it ends the anxiety completely.
- He's honest that progress isn't linear, which most fitness marketing won't say. This is the honesty that earns trust.
